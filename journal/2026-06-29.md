# The World Cup Is Eating Itself (And We're Here For It)

Three matches in and the 2026 World Cup is already tossing the script in the bin. Argentina just dismantled Jordan 3-1, which sounds routine until you remember Jordan shouldn't even be close to this stage. But that's 2026 for you: expanded format, expanded chaos, expanded *everything*.

The real story, though? That 3-3 between Algeria and Austria is the kind of match that makes you understand why people lose sleep over football. You get a scoreline like that in the group stage and suddenly no one knows anything. Algeria, written off by half the world's media, dragging Austria into a slugfest. Both teams walking away with a point feeling like they won the Champions League. That's tournament football at its best: uncertainty, drama, the sense that absolutely anyone could do damage.

Brazil's 2-1 over Japan was exactly what you'd expect from them until it wasn't. Sloppy in patches, clinical when it mattered, with just enough sweat to remind you that even the favorites have to earn their breakfast. Japan pushing them, though? That's the expanded format working exactly as intended. These aren't dead rubber matches anymore. Everyone's got a path.

But let's talk about what's coming. Germany versus Paraguay today, then Netherlands-Morocco tomorrow. You've got Florian Wirtz and the German machine against a Paraguay side with nothing to lose. And Morocco? They've already shown they're not here to make up the numbers. If the Dutch think they're walking into a coronation, they might want to check the Group G standings again.

The expanded World Cup was supposed to dilute quality. Instead, it's creating this beautiful chaos where form matters more than pedigree and a team like Algeria can make a mockery of the odds. Thirty-two teams used to feel like the sweet spot. Maybe we've been wrong about that.

Today's matches will tell us if yesterday's surprises were flukes or the new normal.
